Proposition: Complaints against the NYPD are often ethnically motivated.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Design Choice: Excluding complaints where ethnicity was available
    • Score: 1
    • Rational: Excluding unmatchable records while in theory could fall into either bucket (true or false), there weren't enough unmatched records to change the conclusion of the graph. Mismatched ethnicities still file a significantly large portion of complaints compared to same ethnicity complaints. However, by excluding the unmatchable values the result is even more conservative making the point even more powerful.
  • Design Choice: Showing the percentage of the total complaints versus a count (and only using the matched records in the percentage calculations)
    • Score: .5
    • Rational: There is a lot of value in sharing large shocking numbers which is what the case would be if I showed the total count instead of the percentages. The more honest approach in my eyes was to use percentages which also has the added effect of potentially curtail any suspicions about the conclusions, thus I decided to use the percentages. However, I used the total excluding unmatchable records as the denominator for the percentages. This allowed the two bars to add up to 100%, but can seem a bit dishonest since 75% of complaints were not between two parties of different ethnicities. Just 75% of the cases where we know the ethnicities.
  • Design Choice: Sorting from largest to smallest resulting in False being first and then True
    • Score: -0.5
    • Rational: The nominal baseline for a chart comparing a "True" variable and "False" one would be to have "True" first and then "False" in my mind. I think most people are tuned to have the affirmative/positive value and then the negative alternative which is flipped in this visualization. This was partially driven by wanting to show the larger bar first since the aesthetic of the visual look better to me with the larger bar first. The other driver is to try to associate the user's affirmative/positive mindset associated with the first and larger bar with the proposition. This was to avoid the confusion of associating the "False" bar with negating the proposition.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Design Choice: Including unknown unmatchable records as a separate plot.
    • Score: 0.5
    • Rational: Explicitly including a plot with how the unknown/unmatchable records here reinforces that the complaint resolution time is roughly the same even when we do not know the ethnicities of the parties involved. By showing all of the data it allows the viewer to relax and trust the comprehensiveness of the analysis more.
  • Design Choice: Phrasing the title and subtitle as a question and answer pair
    • Score: 1.5
    • Rational: The design choice was focused on clarity. The very upfront nature of the title and subtitle also implies a very specific way to read and interpret the data. I considered using the same approach as visualization 1, but given there were 3 line graphs next to each other, I felt that the cognitive load would be too high with the False/True dichotomy and decided to guide the reader more directly. This could have an unintended impact through readers questioning why the visualization signposts are so direct.
  • Design Choice: Using the average resolution time as the y-axis and the year closed for x-axis
    • Score: -1
    • Rational: The overall proposition was focused on articulating if there is an ethnically motivated aspect to complaints. This visualization uses average complaint resolution times as a proxy for ethnic motivation. When the plots end up looking the same across scenarios, it claims that ethnicity likely doesn't have a role to play in how complaints are motivated.

Final Reflection

I found it very difficult to pick a proposition that could be supported using two separate visualizations. I decided to start creating calculated parameters/fields combining columns in interesting ways to answer questions that I thought were relevant. When I initially created the "ethnicity match" field I was hoping to make a proposition related to harassment of minorities. However, I couldn't find a proposition/visualization combination that was convincing to me. Thus, I decided to have a relatively general/vague proposition about ethnically motivated complaints. This proposition suffers from a major issue. Primarily, proving motivation from quantitative data is both difficult and likely full of bias. From there both visualizations end up arguing two different variables that could indicate motivation - count and resolution time. The resolution time was another variable that I had to calculate myself and I found interesting. The first plot was able to use a plot that was for the proposition by focusing on the share of complaints being different between scenarios where the ethnicities matched and situations where they did not match. The second visualization focused on resolution time. The implication being that there should be a difference in complaint resolution time between ethnicities if the complaints were actually ethnically motivated, but the plots are basically identical.
